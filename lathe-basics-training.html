Introduction to the Lathe

A lathe is one of the oldest and most essential machine tools. It rotates a workpiece while a cutting tool removes material to shape it — a process called turning. This module covers the major parts, common operations, and fundamental safety rules.

In the following slides you'll learn about each major component, explore common lathe operations, review safety essentials, and check your understanding with a short quiz.

Operations

Common Lathe Operations

A lathe can perform many different machining operations, each producing a specific type of feature or surface finish.

Diameter
Turning
Reduces the outer diameter by feeding the tool parallel to the axis of rotation.
Face
Facing
Creates a flat, smooth surface perpendicular to the workpiece axis.
Bore
Boring
Enlarges and precisely finishes an existing hole using a boring bar.
Thread
Threading
Cuts helical threads on the outside or inside of a workpiece using the lead screw.
Separate
Parting / Cutoff
Severs a finished part from bar stock using a narrow parting blade.
Taper
Taper Turning
Produces a conical surface by angling the compound rest or offsetting the tailstock.
Finish
Knurling
Rolls a cross-hatch grip pattern onto the surface using hardened knurling wheels.
Hole
Drilling
Produces a centre-axis hole by advancing a drill bit mounted in the tailstock.
Safety

Essential Safety Rules

The lathe is powerful machinery. Always follow these rules before and during every operation.

Always wear safety glasses. Chips and coolant can be ejected at high speed. Eye protection is non-negotiable at all times near an operating lathe.
No loose clothing, gloves, or jewellery. Rotating parts can catch fabric or rings and pull hands into the machine instantly.
Remove the chuck key immediately. Never leave the chuck key in the chuck. If the spindle starts with the key in place, it becomes a dangerous projectile.
Never stop the chuck by hand. Allow the spindle to come to a full stop on its own or use the brake. Hand-stopping risks entanglement.
Check clearance before starting. Rotate the chuck by hand to verify no part of the workpiece, tool, or fixture will collide during operation.
Select the correct speed for the material. Too high a speed can cause chatter, tool breakage, or ejection of the workpiece. Consult speed charts for your material and diameter.
Support long stock properly. Bar stock extending more than 2–3× its diameter beyond the chuck must be supported with a tailstock centre or steady rest to prevent dangerous whipping.
